## The Rich Don't Save Money

I need to tell you something that contradicts almost every piece of financial advice you've ever received.

The wealthy do not build wealth by saving money.

They build wealth by **moving** money.

If that sounds wrong, it's because you've been taught to think about money as a static thing. Something you earn, save, and hopefully don't lose. Something that sits in an account and grows slowly over decades. Something you accumulate by spending less than you earn.

That's the poor and middle-class model. And it works ΓÇö barely. Slowly. Painfully.

The wealthy operate on a completely different model. They don't stockpile money. They *deploy* it. They send it out to do work, bring it back with a profit, and send it out again. Over and over and over.

This is the concept of velocity. And understanding it is the difference between spending 40 years grinding toward a modest retirement and building genuine, generational freedom.

## What Is Velocity of Money?

In economics, velocity of money refers to how quickly money changes hands in an economy. A dollar that gets spent five times in a month has higher velocity than a dollar that sits in a savings account for a year.

But Nelson Nash applied this concept to personal finance in a way that changes everything.

Think of your money as an employee. Right now, if you're like most people, your money is working one job. It sits in a savings account earning 0.5%. Or it's locked in a 401(k) growing at the market's mercy. Or it's invested in a rental property generating cash flow.

One job. One paycheck. Just like you.

**Velocity is about putting your money to work at multiple jobs simultaneously.**

Here's how that works with the banking function we discussed in Article 4.

## One Dollar, Multiple Jobs

Let's trace a single dollar through a conventional system versus the IBC system.

**Conventional path:**

You earn $1. After taxes, you keep $0.70. You put it in a savings account. It earns 0.5% annually. After 30 years of compounding, that $0.70 is worth about $0.81.

That dollar worked one job ΓÇö sitting in a savings account ΓÇö for 30 years. And it barely kept up with inflation.

**IBC path:**

You earn $1. After taxes, you keep $0.70. You put it into your whole life policy as premium. It begins compounding in your cash value ΓÇö guaranteed growth plus dividends. Let's say it earns an average of 4-5% over time.

But here's the key: that $0.70 doesn't just sit there.

Year 2: You need to finance a car. You take a policy loan for $0.70. Your cash value *continues to compound* as if you hadn't touched it (because the insurance company lends you their money, collateralized by your cash value). Now your $0.70 is doing *two jobs simultaneously*: compounding inside the policy AND being deployed for a purchase.

You set up a repayment schedule to your policy ΓÇö let's say $0.14/year for 5 years, plus interest. So you're repaying $0.80 over 5 years. That money flows back into your system.

Year 7: You now have more than $0.70 back in accessible capital (the original amount grew while you were using it, and the repayment added to it). You deploy again ΓÇö maybe for a business expense, maybe for another car, maybe for an investment opportunity.

**The same dollar is working three, four, five jobs over 30 years.** Each deployment generates a return. Each return flows back into the system. Each cycle makes the system larger.

After 30 years, that original $0.70? It's been deployed and recaptured multiple times. The cumulative effect isn't $0.81 (savings account). It's multiples of the original amount ΓÇö because the dollar never stopped moving.

That's velocity.

## Why Banks Understand This and You Don't

Banks operate entirely on the principle of velocity. They don't let money sit idle. Ever.

Your deposit of $50,000 is immediately deployed ΓÇö lent to borrowers, invested in bonds, used to fund other operations. When payments come in, that money is immediately redeployed. The bank's entire business model is: receive money, put it to work, collect the return, redeploy.

They do this with *your* money. And they make billions.

Nash asked the most obvious question in the world: **Why don't individuals do the same thing?**

The answer is: because nobody teaches them. The financial industry teaches you to save. To budget. To invest in index funds and wait 40 years. To park your money and hope compound interest does the heavy lifting.

But compound interest at 4-5% with a single deployment is a fraction of what's possible when you add velocity. When your money is working multiple jobs, compounding at each stop, the wealth-building effect multiplies.

This is why Nelson Nash subtitled his book "The Infinite Banking Concept." The word *infinite* doesn't mean unlimited money. It means **infinite cycling.** The money keeps moving. It never stops working. Every dollar, every day, doing something productive.

## The Grocery Store Analogy

Nash loved analogies, and one of his best was the grocery store.

A grocery store doesn't make money by buying food and hoarding it. They make money by **turning inventory.** They buy a can of soup for $0.80, sell it for $1.20, and use the $1.20 to buy more soup. They cycle their inventory as fast as possible.

A grocery store with $100,000 in inventory that turns it 50 times a year processes $5,000,000 in annual sales. The profit isn't in the markup on any single can ΓÇö it's in the *velocity of the turnover.*

Now look at how most people treat their money. They earn it, save it, and let it sit. No turnover. No velocity. No cycling. They're a grocery store that buys inventory and stacks it in the back room hoping it'll be worth more someday.

The wealthy are grocery stores with rapid turnover. They deploy capital, collect returns, redeploy. Their money never sits idle. And the compounding effect of continuous deployment is exponentially more powerful than the compounding effect of a static savings account.

## Volume Over Rate

Here's another insight from Nash that changes how you think about money: **focus on the volume of money moving through your system, not the rate of return.**

The conventional financial world is obsessed with rate. "What's your return?" "Are you beating the S&P?" "What's the interest rate?"

Nash thought this was the wrong question entirely. He cared about volume. How much money is flowing through your banking system? How often? How quickly?

Think about it mathematically. Which is better?

- **Option A:** $100,000 earning 10% per year, deployed once. After one year: $110,000.
- **Option B:** $100,000 earning 5% per cycle, deployed three times in one year. After one year: $100,000 ├ù 1.05 ├ù 1.05 ├ù 1.05 = $115,763.

Option B has a lower rate but higher velocity ΓÇö and it wins.

This is why banks don't care about earning 10% on a single loan. They care about turning their capital as many times as possible. Each turn earns a small spread. But the cumulative effect of many turns at a small spread massively outperforms a single deployment at a high rate.

When you control the banking function in your own life, you adopt this mindset. You stop asking "what's the return?" and start asking "how many times can I cycle this capital?"

## Velocity + Uninterrupted Compounding = The Multiplier

Here's what makes the IBC system uniquely powerful for velocity: **uninterrupted compounding.**

In a normal investment, when you withdraw money to deploy it, you lose the compounding on that money. Pull $50,000 out of your brokerage account to buy rental property, and that $50,000 stops earning returns in the market.

With a whole life policy, when you take a policy loan, your cash value *continues to compound.* The insurance company doesn't subtract from your cash value. They lend you money from their general fund, using your cash value as collateral. Your money stays put, growing.

This means your capital is simultaneously:
1. **Compounding inside the policy** (guaranteed growth + dividends)
2. **Deployed in the real world** (generating returns from whatever you used it for)
3. **Being repaid to your system** (recapturing capital for future deployment)

Three jobs at once. No other financial vehicle allows this. A brokerage account can't do it. A savings account can't do it. Real estate equity can't do it. Only the whole life policy structure provides uninterrupted compounding during deployment.

This is why Nash chose whole life. Not for the rate of return (which looks modest in isolation). For the velocity it enables. When your capital never stops compounding, every deployment is additive. Every cycle makes the system stronger. Every year, the machine gets bigger.
