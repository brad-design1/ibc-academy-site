## Wade Pfau: The Retirement Researcher Who Changed the Conversation

**Wade Pfau, PhD, CFA, RICP** is a professor of retirement income at The American College of Financial Services. He holds a doctorate in economics from Princeton. He's a Chartered Financial Analyst. He's a Retirement Income Certified Professional. He is not an insurance agent. He doesn't sell policies. He publishes peer-reviewed research.

When Pfau turned his attention to whole life insurance, he wasn't trying to validate it. He was trying to understand how different asset classes perform within retirement portfolios—particularly during periods of market stress.

Here's what he found.

**Whole life insurance behaves as a non-correlated asset class.** When stocks drop, whole life cash values don't drop with them. When bonds get crushed by rising interest rates, whole life cash values keep growing at their guaranteed rate plus dividends. This isn't theory. It's observable in the data across multiple market cycles.

Pfau's research on "safety-first" retirement planning demonstrates that allocating a portion of retirement assets to whole life insurance *improves overall portfolio outcomes*. Not because whole life outperforms the S&P 500 in bull markets—it doesn't, and nobody credible claims it does. But because it provides a stable foundation that allows the rest of the portfolio to stay invested during downturns instead of selling at a loss.

This is the sequence-of-returns risk problem. If you retire and the market drops 30% in year one, you're forced to sell investments at depressed prices to fund living expenses. That early damage compounds for decades. Pfau's work shows that having a pool of guaranteed, accessible capital—like whole life cash value—lets retirees draw from a stable source during downturns, leaving their equity portfolio time to recover.

The conventional financial planning world largely ignored this research. Not because it was flawed—Pfau's methodology is rigorous. But because it challenged the orthodoxy that the stock market is the only serious wealth-building tool. When a Princeton PhD with a CFA credential says whole life insurance improves portfolios, it's hard to dismiss. So most people just... don't mention it.

## Robert Murphy: The Economist Who Did the Math

**Robert P. Murphy, PhD** holds a doctorate in economics from New York University. He's the author of multiple economics textbooks, including study guides for Ludwig von Mises' *Human Action*. He's a senior fellow at the Mises Institute. He is an economist first, not an insurance professional.

Murphy came to whole life insurance through Austrian economics—the school of thought that emphasizes individual action, sound money, and the time-value of capital. When he analyzed the mechanics of policy loans, he found something that critics consistently miss.

**The opportunity cost argument actually supports whole life when you account for the banking function.**

Here's what Murphy demonstrated in his Lara-Murphy Report (LMR) articles. Critics calculate "returns" on whole life by comparing premium dollars in to cash value out, then comparing that number to index fund returns. But this calculation ignores what happens *between* those two points.

When you take a policy loan, your cash value continues to earn guaranteed interest and dividends—uninterrupted. Meanwhile, the loan proceeds are deployed elsewhere: buying equipment, financing a car, funding a business opportunity. Your capital is performing *double duty*. The same dollar is working in two places simultaneously.

Murphy's car financing analysis is particularly instructive. He walked through the actual numbers: a whole life policy owner who finances car purchases through policy loans, repaying themselves instead of a bank, recaptures the interest that would otherwise flow to a third-party lender. Over a lifetime of vehicle purchases, the difference isn't marginal. It's substantial—tens of thousands of dollars that stay within the policy owner's system rather than enriching a finance company.

His analysis of policy illustrations and internal rates of return showed that when you factor in the tax-advantaged access to capital, the guaranteed growth, the dividend history of mutual companies, and the death benefit, whole life performs very differently than the caricature critics present.

Murphy didn't come to these conclusions because someone sold him a policy. He came to them because the math led there.

## The Numbers Critics Never Show You

Here's what gets left out of every "whole life is terrible" article and YouTube video:

**Tax-advantaged access to capital.** Cash value in a properly structured, non-MEC whole life policy grows tax-deferred. Policy loans are not taxable events. You access your capital without triggering income tax. Compare that to a 401(k) where every dollar withdrawn in retirement is taxed as ordinary income, or a brokerage account where gains trigger capital gains tax. Tax treatment depends on structure and staying non-MEC—but when structured correctly, this advantage is significant.

**Guaranteed cash value growth.** The guaranteed interest rate in a whole life policy is contractual. It doesn't depend on market performance, economic conditions, or Federal Reserve decisions. It's written into the contract. Find me another financial instrument that offers a contractual guarantee of growth with zero market risk.

**Mutual company dividends.** The major mutual life insurance companies—the ones IBC practitioners use—have paid dividends continuously for over 100 years. Through the Great Depression. Through World War II. Through stagflation, dot-com crashes, the 2008 financial crisis, and a global pandemic. These aren't guaranteed, and past performance doesn't guarantee future results. But a 100-plus year track record of uninterrupted dividends tells you something about the stability of the institution.

**The death benefit as a wealth transfer tool.** Whole life insurance pays a death benefit to beneficiaries income-tax-free under current tax law. This creates an asset that completes its highest function at the moment of death—precisely when every other asset is subject to estate settlement, potential taxation, and family disruption.

**Constant compounding, uninterrupted.** As Robert Murphy explained in his LMR articles, the cash value inside a whole life policy compounds continuously. When you take a policy loan, that compounding doesn't stop. Your cash value keeps earning as if you never touched it. This is the mechanical reality that makes the banking function work—and it's the detail critics either don't understand or don't mention.

## What This Means

Let me be clear about what I'm *not* saying.

I'm not saying whole life insurance is the best investment. It isn't an investment at all—it's a banking tool. I'm not saying everyone should buy it. Whether it fits depends on your goals and funding ability. I'm not saying it replaces stocks, bonds, or real estate.

What I *am* saying is this: when credentialed, independent researchers with no financial incentive to promote whole life insurance study it honestly, they find something different from what the critics claim. Wade Pfau finds it improves retirement portfolios. Robert Murphy finds the economics support the banking function. Carlos Lara documents it working in practice. Nelson Nash demonstrated it across three decades of personal use.

These aren't salespeople. They're researchers, economists, and practitioners who followed the data.
